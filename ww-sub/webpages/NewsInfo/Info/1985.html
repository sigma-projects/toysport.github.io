1985

NINETEEN EIGHTY-FIVE

 

1985.   Toyota could not do anything wrong.  The U.S. product line was solid.  Product for product, Toyota models bested offerings from all manufacturers in the important U.S. market.  The Corolla GTS, MR2, Celica GTS, Supra- Toyotas you were proud to drive. Car of the Year Awards all over the place. Then President of Toyota-  Mr. Togo set his personal goals for the company, and he achieved them all before retiring.

That same year in a talk with Toyota USA’s Japanese Liaison officer, discussion centered on the future of Toyota in the U.S.  Trade magazines were saying that based on market projections Toyota was going to be surpassed by another Japanese maker in five years- he said ‘no, that information is inaccurate because it will happen in 3 years!’  Conversation was based on total car sales only.  It was such a strange prediction, that haunts today.

By 1990, Toyota was still on top- if you added truck and van sales that the other manufacturer did not offer.  Otherwise, the predictions by the trade magazine and the Toyota officer had come true.  Then another revelation- in a visit to Japan to meet some Toyota people- talk was about the rapid expansion in popularity of the other Japanese manufacturer in the U.S.- specially in the entry-level and sporty market. 

There was a reason, they said – Toyota Global Ten.  Global Ten is a Toyota corporate goal for 10% of the world automotive market.  Toyota was ready to do this, but the Japanese Trade Ministry asked Toyota to back off: to help preserve the Japanese economy.  At that time, two big car manufacturers in Japan were at the brink of collapse, which if it happened would have caused Japanese and World recession.

In effect Toyota Japan was ordered to hold expansion.  This resulted in Toyota trying to maximize profit on model lines and unit sales.  There was less emphasis on entry-level, low margin vehicles.  In the European markets, this worked well since the image Toyota was trying to project was for world-class products.  In the U.S. the Turbo Supra and MR2 were offered to the enthusiasts- also best in class.  The entry of Lexus was a very bright spotlight for Toyota, which further dimmed the product line weakness in the entry-level field.  The other Japanese manufacturer continued introducing better entry-level and sporty units.

By 1995, Toyota was still on top of all Imports.  Lexus continued to shine, forcing Europeans to reposition and reinvent their stars, but the Toyota entry models was headed the other direction.  In the U.S., magazine surveys also showed the different Toyota models became also-rans. The “other” Japanese manufacturer had entrenched on the entry-level market firmly. 

In another Japan visit, this issue was discussed again.  Yes, Toyota Corporate in Japan is aware of the U.S. condition, and yes they have formed a group to address this problem. But other business interests were more crucial for Toyotas future- communication, construction, and aviation.  And in Japan- the “other” Japanese manufacturer did not matter, even in the popular Tokyo Auto Salon show.  There were hardly any of their products on display.

The last 5 years into 2000, was very good for Toyota and Lexus.  Record profits and market share.  The New Millennium however called for a total Toyota corporate review and redirection.  It is not too late. 

1985 seemed like a good year.

Toyota, specially in the U.S., has not lost it’s following. They still buy Toyotas to fill the record books.  What Toyota has lost however, is a generation of people interested in CARS (and bringing their cars to places like Toysport).  Cars that are the best in their class.  Cars with state-of-the art engines.  Cars you are proud to drive.  Cars you can have fun in.  And can afford (and still visit Toysport).  HINT: Not goofy, radical ads, for weird cars!  It heightens the shallowness of the product.   Talk to the original owners of the 85 cars.  Talk to current owners of the 85 cars.  Do not be afraid of a little oil and grease.  Smell the exhaust. 

1985 seemed like a good year.
